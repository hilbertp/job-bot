## Web3 / DeFi Focus

I have a deep understanding of how decentralized systems work and where they fail under real-world pressure.

My knowledge includes:

- **How CEXes and Perp DEXes work**
  - how futures, options and perpetuals work and how they differ  
  - how leverage, margin and funding fees work
  - how low-latency execution and easy KYC onboarding are essential to UX
  - how order books and matching engines are modeled after tradition financial work

- **How DEXes work and how they differ from TradFi market making**
  - how automated market makers and liquidity pools behave  
  - how a tx itself creates price impact, especially in smaller pools using the static x*y=k formula  
  - how MEV worsens this effect when using the public mempool through front-running  
  - how this can be mitigated with solutions such as intents, private order flow, or simple RFQ  
  - how aggregators add value through split routing and path optimization  

- **How bridges work and how they introduce risk**
  - how they differ from swaps  
  - how different mechanisms work: messaging, lock/mint, fronting capital, solver/resolver models and auctions  
  - how attacking collateral or abusing faulty messaging can cause large-scale losses  

- **How oracles work**
  - how they connect systems regardless of type  
  - how they mitigate risk through fallback pricing and clear danger signaling  
  - how they guarantee predictability to users through bounding  

I approach these topics with a risk-first lens, focusing on trust assumptions, explicit failure domains, and what happens when incentives weaken or monitoring decays.

Additionally, I have more than eight years of hands-on experience as a retail crypto user: managing hot and cold wallets, interacting with DEXes and aggregators, providing liquidity, and seeing first-hand where products hold up and where they have poor user experience.

## System‑Level Reasoning & Incentive Awareness

I don’t assume I know what a protocol’s real problems are upfront. My value is in identifying where issues *might* emerge when incentives, verification mechanisms and real‑world behavior interact. ("Which drivers are critical to us and what happens when they approach their extreme edges?")

Claiming certainty ahead of time is hybris. What actually works is building clarity of potential futures scenarios and preparing for the time they materialize. Surfacing these risks early, even if they later turn out to be benign, is part of responsible product leadership.

As a PM, my role is not to dictate solutions but structure team communication, keeping them aligned across engineering, operations and support (full DevOps chain) and ensuring decisions/assumptions can be audited, revised and reversed.
